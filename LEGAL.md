The United States legal framework operates as an integrated common law system, guided by the U.S. Constitution and structured into distinct federal and state court systems. 
This adversarial process balances statutory law with historical judicial precedent. Courts ensure that the rule of law is maintained, protecting individual liberties and providing a neutral 
forum to interpret laws while respecting the separation of powers.

The American justice system harmonizes societal order (the law) with fairness (kindness/equity) through several core 
functions:

**Constitutional Supremacy:** The U.S. Supreme Court and subordinate federal courts evaluate laws against the Constitution, ensuring that both legislation and executive 
actions protect minority rights and fundamental freedoms.

**Dual Jurisdiction:** A balance of power exists between state and federal courts, where state court systems manage localized 
civil and criminal matters while federal courts handle issues involving federal statutes or interstate disputes.

**The Common Law Tradition:** Relying on past judicial decisions, judges apply established legal precedents (stare decisis) to provide consistency and predictability, ensuring cases are judged fairly over time.

**Fair Adjudication:** Through adversarial trials, opposing counsel present evidence to impartial judges and juries. This process safeguards the due process of law for all individuals, 
fostering respect for the judiciary as a neutral protector of rights.
